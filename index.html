About F1

Formula One, also called F1 in short, is an international auto racing sport. F1 is the highest level of single-seat, open-wheel and open-cockpit professional motor racing contest. Formula One racing is governed and sanctioned by a world body called the FIA − Fédération Internationale de l'Automobile or the International Automobile Federation. The name ‘Formula’ comes from the set of rules that the participating cars and drivers must follow.


Things you neeed to know

There are a few things you need to know to understand this sport better. Below this text you can learn about them.

The minimum total distance of a Grand Prix race, including all the pre-defined number of laps must be 300 km or 190 miles. This is the standard distance for all races except for the Monaco GP which is 260 km or 160 miles.

10 teams with two cars each are permitted to compete in the F1 World Championship as of 2015. That is, a total of 20 cars can enter the competition. However, the FIA regulations allow a limit of 26 cars for the championship.

The results of all the Grand Prix races in a season are taken together to determine two annual Championship awards. They are:
• Drivers' Championship Award (for the drivers)
• Constructors' Championship Award (for the constructors)

Every F1 car is composed of two main components − the chassis and the engine. Chassis − Formula One cars these days are made from carbon fiber and ultra-lightweight components. The weight must be not less than 702 kg or 1548 lbs, including the driver and tires, but excluding the fuel. The dimensions of a Formula One car must be maximum 180 cm (width) × 95cm (height); there is no specified number for maximum length, but all cars tend to be of almost the same length. Engine − According to regulation changes in 2014, all F1 cars must deploy 1.6 liter turbocharged V6 engines.

Gearbox Semi-automatic sequential carbon titanium gearboxes are used by F1 cars presently, with 8 forward gears and 1 reverse gear, with rear-wheel drive. Steering Wheel The steering wheel of an F1 car is equipped to perform many functions like changing gears, changing brake pressure, calling the radio, fuel adjustment, and so on. Fuel The fuel used by Formula One cars is a tightly controlled mixture of ordinary petrol, and can only contain commercial gasoline compounds rather than alcohol compounds. Tires Formula One cars have been using smooth thread, slick tires since 2009. The tire dimensions of an F1 car are −
• Front Tire − 245mm (width)
• Rear Tires − 355mm and 380mm (width) Brakes Formula One cars use disc brakes with a rotor and caliper at each tire.

All F1 cars can accelerate from 0 to 100 mph (160 kmph) and decelerate back to 0 in under 5 seconds. F1 cars have reached top speeds of about 300 kmph or 185 mph on an average. However, some cars, without fully complying with F1 standards have attained speed of 400 kmph or more. These numbers are mostly same for all F1 cars but slight variations may be there due to the gears and aerodynamics configuration.

Safety is high priority in motor sports. Formula One has seen many tragedies in its early days with many casualties that included drivers and spectators as cars crashed at high speeds. Engineers researched using latest technology to build safer cars and gear for drivers. This has certainly reduced the number of incidents over the last decade. Let’s know about the different safety gears used by drivers in this chapter. Drivers have to use light, fire resistant helmets which meets FIA standarts. HANS whith stands for Head and Neck Support, it protects drivers heads in case of collision or accident. And thirdly - clothing. The clothing of F1 drivers is designed to protect them from fire accidents in case of crash. It is a multilayered suit that matches specifications of NASA.

107% rule − A driver should not exceed 107 percent of fastest time in qualifying session; if he fails to do so, then he is not allowed to start the race. He is allowed to start if he sets a suitable time during the practice session.
Backmarker − The drivers lagging behind are called backmarkers. They are shown blue flag to give way for leading drivers.
Blistering − Improper selection of tire compound can cause excess heat that can lead to chunks of rubber breaking off from the tire. This is called blistering. Cockpit − The seating area of the driver in a F1 car is called the cockpit.
Delta Time − The difference of time between two laps or two cars is referred to as delta time.
Drive-through penalty − A penalty imposed for minor offence or violation of rules on track. It requires the driver to drive at a minimum speed and enter the pit lane without stopping. The driver is allowed to re-join the race.
Flat spot − The portion of the tire where it is heavily worn out due to spin or extreme breaking is called the flat spot.
Formation lap or warm-up lap − Cars are driven for a lap from the grid before the start of the race and then get back to grid positions. This is known as the warm-up lap.
Jump start − Sensors are installed at grid positions to monitor the cars that start before all the red lights go off signaling the start of race. Such start is called jump start and the driver earns a penalty for it.
Lollipop − The sign board at the pit stop that signals the driver to apply brakes and stay in first gear before the car is lowered from jacks.
Parc ferme − A restricted area where no team members are allowed and all race cars are driven here after completion of practice or race. This area is supervised by race stewards only.
Pit board − A board held at pit wall to inform the driver about delta time, remaining number of laps, and current position.
Pit wall − It is F1 team area where the manager, engineers, and support staff keep close watch on their cars using small screens under a shelter.
Pits − An area of the track separated by a wall. Cars enter team’s pit garage for change of wheels, refuel the car, and set up other changes.
Pole position − Driver who recorded fastest time during qualifying session is awarded the first grid position on race day.
Steward − A high-ranked official appointed for races to make decisions.
Tire warmer − An electronic blanket is wrapped around the tires before cars are positioned for race that keeps them at optimum temperatures before the start of the race.
Visor strip − The top edge of helmet needs extra protection and it is provided with a visor strip made of Zylon that is a high-resistant carbon fiber material.
Constructor − Since 1981, FIA has passed a rule that respective F1 teams have to build their own engine and chassis of the car. The owner of the engine and chassis is called the constructor.
Circuit − Formula One circuits are tracks specifically and purposefully built for conducting races. F1 circuits are of two types − Street Circuit and Road Circuit DRS - stands for Drag Reduction System. It is an aerodynamic device that is used to reduce drag on a car's rear wing, thereby increasing its top speed.

Similar to motor sports and other racing competitions, racing flags are used in Formula One to send out various messages and signals to the drivers like the race start or finish, lap indication, bad weather indication, etc.
Three categories of flags are used in F1 racing. They are
-Status Flags
-Instruction Flags
-The Chequered Flag
There are 5 status flags
Green flag - indicates the start of a race or a restart
Yellow flag - an indication of caution to the drivers to go slow due to some accident or hazard on the track
Red flag - an indication of danger, drivers have to stop or return to the pit immediately
Red & Yellow stripped flag - It indicates that the conditions of the track have been modified by car debris and could cause problems like loss of control and reduced grip.
White flag - It is displayed at the end of free practice sessions (Friday & Saturday) on the last corner and pit straight, indicating to the drivers that other drivers are engaged in practice on pit straight.
Instuction flags
Instruction flags are meant to only one driver at a time
Black Flag − It is shown to impose penalty when a driver has broken some rules and instruct the driver to get back to the pit.
Black Flag with Orange Circle − It also indicates that a car is being asked to return to the cockpit due to technical problems like fuel leak, water leak or oil leak, that might interfere with the proceedings of the race.
Per-bend black/white flag − One diagonal of this flag is black and the other diagonal is white and is an indication of penalty on the driver due to lack of sportsmanship behavior.
Black flag with white cross − This flag is black with two white diagonal crosses and is waved when a driver ignores other black flags and instructs the driver that his car is not scoring anymore.
Blue Flag − It indicates to the driver that another faster car is coming towards his direction and that he should make way for the faster car.
The Chequered flag
The chequered flag is waved at the finish line, indicating that the race is officially over.

A Formula One Grand Prix takes place over a weekend− Friday, Saturday and Sunday. Different events take place on each of these 3 days. They are
Friday - FP1 and FP2 (Free Practice 1 and 2) sessions
Saturday - FP3 plus Qualifying sessions
Sunday - Race day
Free Practice sessions each are 90 minutes (FP3 60 minutes), one is in the mornings and the second on in the afternoon.
Qualifying sessions determinates the starting order of the race day. Qualifying lasts 1 hour and is divided into 3 knock out stages - Q1, Q2, Q3. Q1 lasts 18 minutes and after the 5 slowest cars are eliminated. Q2 lasts 15 minutes and againthe 5 slowest cars are eliminated. Q3 lasts 12 minutes the fastest driver occupies 'pole position'
Race day is the main event of Grand Prix

History

The roots of modern Formula One can be traced back to the Grand Prix motor racing events that began in France in the early 1900s. These events were held on public roads and featured high-performance cars from various manufacturers. After World War II, the Fédération Internationale de l'Automobile (FIA) was founded to oversee international motorsport racing, and in 1946 it established the first World Championship for drivers. The first race of the championship was held at Silverstone in 1950, and the championship has been held every year since then.

Here is a brief history of Formula One:

  • 1950: The first official Formula One World Championship season is held
  • 1958: The first Constructors' Championship is introduced, which awards points to teams based on their finishing positions in each race.
  • 1961: The first use of a fully automatic gearbox in F1, by the British racing team Lotus.
  • 1962: The use of mid-engined cars becomes mandatory, which significantly improves the handling and performance of the cars
  • 1969: The first race held outside of Europe, the United States Grand Prix at Watkins Glen.
  • 1975: The introduction of the first in-car camera, allowing viewers to see the driver's perspective during the race.
  • 1978: Bernie Ecclestone becomes the head of the Formula One Constructors Association (FOCA), which represents the interests of the teams.
  • 1982: The first use of a safety car, which is deployed during races to slow down the field in the event of an accident or other disruption.
  • 1994: The death of Ayrton Senna at the San Marino Grand Prix leads to significant changes in safety standards and the introduction of new regulations aimed at reducing speeds and improving driver protection.
  • 1995: Michael Schumacher wins his second Formula One World Championship, beginning a dominant period for the German driver.
  • 2000: Schumacher wins his third title in a row, and goes on to win five more in the following years, becoming the most successful driver in F1 history.
  • 2005: The introduction of a new points system, which awarded points to the top eight finishers instead of just the top six.
  • 2010: Sebastian Vettel becomes the youngest Formula 1 drivers' champion in history at ahe of 23 years and 133 days
  • 2014: The introduction of new, more fuel-efficient hybrid engines, which replaced the previously used V8 engines.
  • 2016: Max Verstappen becomes the youngest ever to win a Grand Prix at age 18 years and 227 days
  • 2020: The COVID-19 pandemic disrupts the Formula One season, leading to the cancellation or postponement of many races and the introduction of new safety protocols to protect drivers and teams.
  • 2020: Lewis Hamilton wins his seventh Formula One World Championship, equalling the record set by Michael Schumacher.

Teams

Red Bull Racing

Red Bull Racing is a Formula One racing team that is based in Milton Keynes, United Kingdom. The team was founded in 2005 and is owned by the Austrian beverage company Red Bull. Red Bull Racing competes in the Formula One World Championship and has won five Constructors' Championships and six Drivers' Championships since its debut in 2005. The team's drivers have included several notable names in the world of motorsports, including Sebastian Vettel, Daniel Ricciardo, Max Verstappen, and Sergio Perez. The team is known for its aggressive and innovative approach to racing, as well as its close relationship with engine supplier Honda. Red Bull Racing has a reputation for being one of the most successful and influential teams in modern Formula One history, and their unique approach to racing has made them a fan favorite around the world.
Team Principal: Christian Horner

Scuderia Ferrari

Scuderia Ferrari is a Formula One racing team that is based in Maranello, Italy. The team was founded in 1929 by Enzo Ferrari and has been competing in the Formula One World Championship since its inception in 1950. Ferrari is the oldest and most successful team in the sport's history, having won 16 Constructors' Championships and 15 Drivers' Championships. Ferrari is known for its iconic red cars and its passionate and dedicated fan base, known as the Tifosi. The team has had some of the most famous drivers in the sport's history, including Michael Schumacher, Niki Lauda, and Juan Manuel Fangio. Ferrari's success and legacy in Formula One have made it one of the most recognizable brands in the world of motorsports, and the team's iconic logo is widely recognized as a symbol of excellence and performance. Despite a recent dip in form, Ferrari remains one of the most popular and influential teams in the sport, and its continued success is eagerly anticipated by fans around the world.
Team Principal:Fred Vasseur

Mercedes-AMG Petronas

Mercedes AMG Petronas is a Formula One racing team that is based in Brackley, United Kingdom. The team was founded in 2010 as Mercedes GP, after the German car manufacturer purchased the championship-winning Brawn GP team. Mercedes AMG Petronas has since become one of the most successful teams in the sport, winning eight consecutive Constructors' Championships from 2014 to 2021. The team's drivers have included some of the most talented and successful racers in the sport's history, including Lewis Hamilton, Nico Rosberg, and Valtteri Bottas. Hamilton has won seven Drivers' Championships while driving for Mercedes, becoming the most successful driver in the sport's history. Mercedes AMG Petronas' dominance in recent years has made it one of the most successful and influential teams in the sport, and its continued success is eagerly anticipated by fans around the world.
